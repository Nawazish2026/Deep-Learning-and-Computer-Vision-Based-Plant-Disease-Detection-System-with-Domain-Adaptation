                                   ABSTRACT

Agricultural productivity is key to global food security, yet crop yields are heavily degraded by plant diseases. In India alone, annual crop losses are estimated to be between 15% and 20% due to undetected or late-diagnosed plant pathologies. Traditional diagnosis relies on physical inspections by agronomists, which is slow, error-prone, and inaccessible in remote rural areas. To address these challenges, this project introduces "AgriVision 2.0," an end-to-end web-based system combining a custom Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture called "AgriNet" with a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) agricultural chat engine.

Standard agricultural computer vision systems are typically trained on controlled laboratory datasets, resulting in a significant "domain gap" and high failure rates when deployed under messy, real-world field conditions characterized by complex backgrounds, shadows, and varying lighting. In this work, this issue is resolved using Mixed-Domain Dataset Fusion and Domain Adaptation. The base model is trained on 54,305 laboratory images from the PlantVillage dataset across 38 classes, achieving 98.75% validation accuracy. To bridge the domain gap, a secondary field-based dataset (PlantDoc, containing 2,231 images) is integrated by mapping 27 real-world classes into the standard 38-class structure. Fine-tuning the network jointly on these combined domains yields a robust mixed-domain validation accuracy of 96.14%.

Crucially, instead of using heavy, off-the-shelf networks, a custom, lightweight CNN backbone is built from scratch. It utilizes Inverted Residual Blocks and Depthwise Separable Convolutions to reduce parameter count and inference latency, enabling deployment on resource-constrained platforms. Explainability is integrated using Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM), which highlights the diagnostic regions of interest on the leaf. 

To complement the classification pipeline, a local knowledge-grounded RAG engine is developed. It splits authoritative agricultural manuals into vector embeddings stored in a local FAISS database. User queries retrieve context-relevant treatments, which are synthesized using an LLM (Llama-3.1-8b-instant) via the Groq API, preventing hallucinations and providing verified treatment recommendations. The system is deployed as a FastAPI backend integrated with a premium Next.js frontend featuring glassmorphic designs, user prediction logs, and downloadable PDF reports.

Keywords: Computer Vision, Deep Learning, Custom CNN, Inverted Residuals, Domain Adaptation, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Explainable AI.

                            CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.0 General
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
Agriculture stands as the backbone of the global economy, providing food, raw materials, and employment for billions of people. In developing nations such as India, agricultural activities employ more than 50% of the workforce and contribute significantly to the national Gross Domestic Product (GDP). However, agricultural yield is constantly threatened by various environmental factors, pests, and plant diseases. Plant pathogens, including fungi, bacteria, viruses, and environmental deficiencies, cause severe damage to crops, leading to an annual loss of approximately 15% to 20% of agricultural production in India. 

Early detection and precise identification of plant diseases are critical to mitigating these losses. Traditionally, farmers diagnose plant diseases based on visual inspection of the symptoms on plant leaves. This manual diagnosis method is highly subjective, time-consuming, and prone to error. Furthermore, expert advice from plant pathologists is often unavailable in remote agricultural sectors. Consequently, incorrect or delayed diagnoses lead to improper usage of chemical pesticides, which increases production costs, damages soil health, and leads to environmental pollution.

In recent years, the integration of Computer Vision (CV) and Deep Learning (DL) has emerged as a promising solution to automate the disease detection process. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have shown exceptional performance in image classification tasks. However, most existing models are developed under controlled laboratory conditions, failing to perform accurately in real-world agricultural fields. This project, entitled "AgriVision 2.0," addresses this domain gap by developing a custom, lightweight CNN architecture named "AgriNet," coupled with domain adaptation techniques, explainable AI capabilities, and a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system for agricultural treatment advice.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.1 Literature Survey
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
To establish the research context, a thorough survey of literature spanning traditional machine learning, deep learning architectures, domain adaptation, and retrieval systems in agriculture was performed.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.1.1 Traditional Classifiers vs. Deep Learning Models
Prior to the dominance of Deep Learning, computer vision in agriculture relied on handcrafted features (such as texture, shape, and color histograms) paired with classical classifiers.
- Sarunyagate et al. [1] and other early researchers proposed image segmentation techniques to isolate lesion areas on leaves, followed by feature extraction. These features were classified using Support Vector Machines (SVM), Random Forests, or K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN).
- A major limitation of these systems was their dependency on manual feature selection, which is highly sensitive to changes in leaf color, size, and lighting conditions.
- With the breakthrough of AlexNet and subsequent models like ResNet and VGG16 [3], the paradigm shifted to Deep Learning. CNNs automate the feature extraction process by learning hierarchical representations directly from raw image pixels.
- However, standard deep learning models like VGG16 are computationally heavy, containing over 138 million parameters, making them unsuitable for real-time mobile deployment or execution on low-power devices in fields.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.1.2 Domain Adaptation in Agricultural Computer Vision
The availability of standardized datasets has accelerated deep learning research. The most prominent dataset is PlantVillage [2], introduced by Hughes and Salathé, which contains over 54,000 images captured in controlled laboratory settings with uniform grey backgrounds.
- Models trained on PlantVillage regularly report classification accuracies exceeding 98%. However, Mohanty et al. demonstrated that when these models are tested on images from actual farm fields, their accuracy drops to under 30%.
- This drop is caused by the "domain gap" (covariate shift), where the background, lighting, and leaf positioning in the target domain (fields) differ significantly from the source domain (laboratory).
- Singh et al. [5] introduced the PlantDoc dataset to represent real-world conditions. Researchers have since utilized domain adaptation techniques, such as mixed-domain training and adversarial training, to enforce the extraction of domain-invariant features. By training models on a combination of clean and noisy images, the networks learn to ignore the background noise and focus strictly on disease lesions.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.1.3 Knowledge-Grounded Support and RAG Systems
A complete agricultural assistant must go beyond classification; it must provide reliable treatment advice to farmers.
- Traditional chatbots and Large Language Models (LLMs) suffer from "hallucinations," generating confident but incorrect treatment advice, which can lead to crop damage if implemented by farmers.
- Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), proposed by Lewis et al. [9], resolves this issue by constraining the LLM's response generation to a set of retrieved documents.
- In agricultural systems, RAG indexes official farming manuals and research guides using vector databases like FAISS [13]. When a user queries the system, relevant paragraphs are retrieved and passed to the LLM to synthesize a grounded, accurate response.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.2 Motivation
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The primary motivation for this project is to address the limitations of existing agricultural classification systems. Standard academic projects often rely on fine-tuning pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet50 or EfficientNet) using transfer learning. While effective, this approach does not demonstrate custom architectural design, which is a key requirement for B.Tech Major Projects. 

Furthermore, farmers are hesitant to trust AI predictions without understanding the reasoning behind them. The black-box nature of deep neural networks necessitates explainable AI (XAI) tools like Grad-CAM [8] to visualize the decision-making process. Finally, there is a clear need for a unified platform that not only detects the disease but also offers verified, non-hallucinated treatment guidelines directly sourced from reliable agricultural publications.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.3 Objectives of the Present Work
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The objectives defined for the design and deployment of the AgriVision 2.0 system are:
1. To design and implement a custom, lightweight CNN architecture ("AgriNet") from scratch in PyTorch, avoiding reliance on pre-trained torchvision architectures.
2. To implement a Mixed-Domain Training pipeline combining laboratory (PlantVillage) and real-world field (PlantDoc) datasets to overcome the lab-to-field domain gap.
3. To achieve a validation accuracy exceeding 95% on mixed-domain validation sets.
4. To integrate Grad-CAM to highlight the areas of interest (lesions) on the crop leaves, making predictions explainable.
5. To construct a local, knowledge-grounded RAG engine using FAISS and Llama-3.1 to retrieve and generate verified treatment recommendations from crop manuals.
6. To develop a web-based portal utilizing Next.js for the UI and FastAPI for backend serving, complete with database persistence for prediction histories.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
1.4 Project Outline
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The remainder of this report is organized as follows:
- Chapter 2 describes the dataset characteristics, preprocessing steps, data augmentation techniques, and the class mapping/fusion pipeline.

           CHAPTER 2: DATASET PREPARATION AND DOMAIN ADAPTATION

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
2.0 General
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
A deep learning model is only as robust as the data it is trained on. For agricultural computer vision applications, model generalization depends on exposure to diverse imaging conditions. This chapter details the datasets used, the preprocessing steps, the data augmentation techniques, and the mapping pipeline implemented to fuse laboratory and field images.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
2.1 Primary Dataset: PlantVillage
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The foundation of the training pipeline is the PlantVillage dataset [2]. It contains 54,305 high-resolution color images of healthy and diseased crop leaves. The dataset spans 14 distinct crop species: Apple, Blueberry, Cherry, Corn, Grape, Orange, Peach, Pepper, Potato, Raspberry, Soybean, Squash, Strawberry, and Tomato. The images are categorized into 38 distinct classes (e.g., 'Tomato_Late_blight', 'Apple_black_rot', 'Corn_Common_rust', and various 'healthy' classes). 

Crucially, all PlantVillage images were captured under controlled laboratory conditions, where individual leaves were placed flat against a uniform, solid grey background under standardized studio lighting. While this setup allows the model to learn the primary morphological features of leaf pathologies, it limits the model's ability to generalize to natural outdoor backgrounds.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
2.2 Secondary Dataset: PlantDoc
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
To introduce real-world noise, the PlantDoc dataset [5] is incorporated. It contains 2,231 images across 13 species. Unlike PlantVillage, PlantDoc images are captured in actual agricultural fields using mobile devices. The images depict leaves in their natural state, attached to the plants, and include complex outdoor backgrounds such as soil, weeds, shadows, varying sunlight, insects, and multiple overlapping leaves. Integrating this dataset allows the network to learn feature representations that are invariant to background clutter.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
2.3 Preprocessing and Augmentation
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
All images from both datasets are subjected to a standardized preprocessing pipeline. The images are resized to 224x224 pixels to match the input dimensions of the AgriNet backbone. Next, pixel values are scaled to the range [0, 1] and normalized using the ImageNet channel-wise mean and standard deviation:
- Mean: $\mu = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406]$
- Standard Deviation: $\sigma = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]$

During the training phase, data augmentation is applied to the training split to prevent overfitting and improve generalization:
1. **RandomHorizontalFlip**: Simulates mirror leaf images.
2. **RandomRotation($\pm20^\circ$)**: Simulates variations in camera rotation.
3. **ColorJitter**: Randomly adjusts brightness (0.2), contrast (0.2), saturation (0.2), and hue (0.1) to simulate varying time-of-day illumination conditions (direct sunlight, cloudy skies, shade).

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
2.4 Mixed-Domain Dataset Fusion Pipeline
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The primary challenge of fusing PlantVillage and PlantDoc is the difference in class folder naming conventions. To resolve this, a custom dataset class, `MappedPlantDocDataset`, was developed in PyTorch. It maps the labels of the secondary dataset to the exact indices of the primary 38-class dataset. 

A mapping dictionary, `PLANTDOC_TO_PV_MAP`, was constructed. It successfully mapped 26 out of the 27 PlantDoc classes to their matching PlantVillage indices (e.g., 'tomato leaf late blight' was mapped to index 33, corresponding to 'Tomato___Late_blight'). One class, 'Tomato mold leaf', was excluded as it lacked a direct biological counterpart in the PlantVillage classes. After mapping, the primary and secondary datasets were joined using PyTorch's `ConcatDataset` class, yielding a unified training corpus of 56,536 images.

Table 2.1: Dataset Distribution and Class Mapping Summary
| Dataset | Image Characteristics | Size (Images) | Number of Classes | Mapping Status |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **PlantVillage** | Controlled Lab, Grey Background | 54,305 | 38 | Reference labels |
| **PlantDoc** | Real-world Field, Noisy Background | 2,231 | 27 | 26 mapped, 1 skipped |
| **Merged Corpus**| Mixed-Domain Dataset | 56,536 | 38 | Fused via ConcatDataset |

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
2.5 Conclusion
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
Through the execution of this fusion pipeline, the domain discrepancy between laboratory-captured and field-captured images is addressed. This prepared dataset forms the basis for training the robust classification model described in the following chapter.

                        CHAPTER 3: PROPOSED METHODOLOGY

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.0 General
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
This chapter presents the architectural design of the custom CNN backbone "AgriNet," the mathematical foundations of its primary building blocks, the two-phase training strategy, and the implementation details of the local Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) agricultural advising system.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.1 Custom CNN Backbone: AgriNet
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
To meet the constraints of mobile deployment, AgriNet is designed as a lightweight, custom Convolutional Neural Network. Rather than using standard, parameter-heavy convolutions, the core backbone of AgriNet is constructed using Depthwise Separable Convolutions and Inverted Residual Blocks (referred to as "AgriBlocks").

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.1.1 Depthwise Separable Convolutions
Depthwise separable convolutions reduce the computational cost and model size by breaking a standard convolution down into two separate steps:
1. **Depthwise Convolution**: Applies a single spatial filter per input channel.
2. **Pointwise Convolution**: Applies a 1x1 convolution to linearly combine the outputs of the depthwise convolution across all channels.

Let $D_K$ be the spatial dimension of the convolutional kernel, $M$ be the number of input channels, $N$ be the number of output channels, and $D_F \times D_F$ be the spatial dimension of the output feature map.

The computational cost of a standard convolution is:
$$\text{Cost}_{\text{standard}} = D_K \cdot D_K \cdot M \cdot N \cdot D_F \cdot D_F$$

The computational cost of a depthwise separable convolution is:
$$\text{Cost}_{\text{separable}} = D_K \cdot D_K \cdot M \cdot D_F \cdot D_F + M \cdot N \cdot D_F \cdot D_F$$

The ratio of computational reduction is expressed as:
$$\frac{\text{Cost}_{\text{separable}}}{\text{Cost}_{\text{standard}}} = \frac{D_K^2 \cdot M \cdot D_F^2 + M \cdot N \cdot D_F^2}{D_K^2 \cdot M \cdot N \cdot D_F^2} = \frac{1}{N} + \frac{1}{D_K^2}$$

For a standard $3 \times 3$ convolution ($D_K = 3$), this formulation reduces the computational parameters and floating-point operations (FLOPs) by approximately 8 to 9 times with only a minimal loss in classification accuracy.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.1.2 Inverted Residual Block (AgriBlock)
The core computational unit of AgriNet is the `AgriBlock`. The block structure consists of three sequential operations:
1. **Expansion Layer**: A $1\times1$ pointwise convolution that increases the channel dimension of the input feature map by an expansion factor ($t$) to project the features into a higher-dimensional space.
2. **Depthwise Layer**: A $3\times3$ depthwise convolution with stride $s$ that performs spatial filtering on the expanded feature space.
3. **Projection Layer**: A $1\times1$ pointwise convolution that projects the filtered features back into a lower-dimensional space. To preserve linear information, no non-linear activation function is applied after this projection (linear bottleneck).

A residual skip connection is applied to connect the input of the block directly to its output, but only when the stride is 1 and the input channel dimension matches the output channel dimension ($s = 1$ and $C_{\text{in}} = C_{\text{out}}$).

The overall network architecture starts with an initial convolutional stem, followed by four stacked AgriBlocks with increasing channel depths, a Global Average Pooling (GAP) layer, a Dropout layer ($p = 0.3$), and a final fully connected classification layer.

Table 3.1: AgriNet Architectural Layout
| Layer | Type | Input Size | Output Size | Kernel | Stride | Expansion ($t$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Stem** | Conv2d + BN + ReLU6 | $3\times224\times224$ | $32\times112\times112$ | $3\times3$ | 2 | - |
| **Block 1**| AgriBlock | $32\times112\times112$ | $64\times112\times112$ | $3\times3$ | 1 | 4 |
| **Block 2**| AgriBlock | $64\times112\times112$ | $128\times56\times56$ | $3\times3$ | 2 | 6 |
| **Block 3**| AgriBlock | $128\times56\times56$  | $256\times28\times28$ | $3\times3$ | 2 | 6 |
| **Block 4**| AgriBlock | $256\times28\times28$  | $512\times14\times14$ | $3\times3$ | 2 | 6 |
| **Pool** | Adaptive Avg Pool2d | $512\times14\times14$  | $512\times1\times1$   | - | - | - |
| **FC**   | Dropout(0.3) + Linear| 512 | 38 | - | - | - |

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.2 Model Training Strategy
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The model training is executed in two consecutive phases.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.2.1 Phase 1: Base Model Training Loop
In the first phase, the model is trained exclusively on the clean, laboratory-based PlantVillage dataset.
- The training loop is configured for 10 epochs with a batch size of 16.
- The Adam optimizer is utilized with an initial learning rate of 0.001.
- Cross-Entropy Loss serves as the loss function.
- The `ReduceLROnPlateau` scheduler is configured to monitor the validation loss, automatically halving the learning rate (factor=0.5, patience=2) when validation performance stagnates.
- The best-performing weights are saved as `agrinet_v1.pth` based on the lowest validation loss.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.2.2 Phase 2: Mixed-Domain Fine-Tuning Loop
In the second phase, the weights from `agrinet_v1.pth` are loaded. The training dataset is swapped for the merged PlantVillage and mapped PlantDoc dataset.
- To prevent catastrophic forgetting of the features learned in Phase 1, the model is fine-tuned with a significantly smaller learning rate of 0.0001.
- The network is trained for an additional 10 epochs.
- The best mixed-domain weights are saved as `agrinet_mixed_v1.pth` for final API serving.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.3 Knowledge-Grounded RAG Chat Engine
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
To provide verified treatment recommendations, the system implements a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.3.1 Document Ingestion and Chunking Strategy
Authoritative agricultural manuals (`apple.pdf`, `potato.pdf`, `tomato.pdf`, `maize.pdf`) are loaded. The text contents are extracted and processed using LangChain's `RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter`. The splitter is configured with a chunk size of 1,000 characters and a chunk overlap of 200 characters.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.3.2 Vector Database and Retrieval Mechanics
The text chunks are converted into dense vector representations using the `sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2` embedding model, yielding a 384-dimensional vector space. The generated embeddings are stored in a local FAISS index. During runtime, when a user asks a question, the query is embedded and the FAISS index retrieves the top 3 most similar chunks based on cosine similarity.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.3.3 Context-Grounded Generation (Groq Llama-3.1)
The retrieved context chunks are combined with the user's query and formatted into a system prompt. This prompt is sent to the `llama-3.1-8b-instant` model hosted on the Groq API. The model generates responses based *only* on the provided context. If the context does not contain the answer, the model states that it does not know. The final generated response is returned to the user along with citations of the source document and page number.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
3.4 Conclusion
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
By combining a lightweight CNN classifier with a localized RAG engine, the proposed methodology addresses both disease identification and treatment advisory within a single, integrated architecture.

4.0 General
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
This chapter outlines the system design of the AgriVision 2.0 application. It details the Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs), the system architecture, database design, and software/hardware requirements.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
4.1 Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs)
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
Data Flow Diagrams model how information flows through the AgriVision 2.0 system.

- **Level 0 (Context Diagram)**:
  At the context level, the system acts as a single process interacting with two external entities: the Farmer/User and the Agricultural PDF Document Database.
  - The User inputs leaf images, login credentials, and disease-related queries.
  - The System returns class predictions, Grad-CAM heatmaps, user prediction histories, and RAG-generated treatment recommendations.
  - The PDF Database serves as the static text corpus used to initialize the RAG knowledge base.

- **Level 1 (Detailed Data Flow)**:
  The Level 1 diagram decomposes the system into three main sub-processes:
  1. **Process 1 (Authentication)**: Manages registration and login. It verifies user credentials against the PostgreSQL `User` table and returns a JWT token.
  2. **Process 2 (Disease Prediction)**: Receives uploaded leaf images via a POST request, preprocesses the images, runs inference using the AgriNet model, generates a Grad-CAM heatmap, and saves the prediction record to the `Prediction` table.
  3. **Process 3 (RAG Chat)**: Receives text queries, embeds them, queries the FAISS index to retrieve document context, and uses the Groq LLM to return a grounded response.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
4.2 Project Architecture
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The system is built as a decoupled client-server architecture:
- **Frontend Layer**: Developed using Next.js 14 and React. It features a responsive layout styled with custom CSS to implement glassmorphism (semi-transparent backgrounds and blurred overlays). Page transitions are animated using Framer Motion. The frontend includes a dashboard where users can upload images, view prediction history, download PDF reports, and chat with the RAG assistant.
- **Backend API Layer**: Developed using FastAPI in Python. It handles HTTP requests asynchronously. The backend loads the `agrinet_mixed_v1.pth` weights on startup and coordinates the classification, Grad-CAM, and RAG pipelines.
- **Database Layer**: PostgreSQL stores user data and prediction logs. It is managed using the Prisma ORM.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
4.3 Database and Schema Design
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The relational database schema is designed to track user authentication and maintain prediction histories. The schema includes two main tables:

1. **User Table**:
   - `id`: Integer, Primary Key, Auto-increment.
   - `name`: String, storing the user's name.
   - `email`: String, Unique, storing the user's email address.
   - `password`: String, storing the hashed password.
   - `createdAt`: DateTime, tracking account creation.

2. **Prediction Table**:
   - `id`: Integer, Primary Key, Auto-increment.
   - `userId`: Integer, Foreign Key referencing `User.id`.
   - `imagePath`: String, storing the relative path of the uploaded image.
   - `disease`: String, storing the predicted disease name.
   - `confidence`: Float, storing the prediction confidence score.
   - `createdAt`: DateTime, tracking the prediction timestamp.

The relations are represented in the Prisma Schema syntax below:
```prisma
model User {
  id          Int          @id @default(autoincrement())
  name        String
  email       String       @unique
  password    String
  createdAt   DateTime     @default(now())
  predictions Prediction[]
}

model Prediction {
  id         Int      @id @default(autoincrement())
  userId     Int
  user       User     @relation(fields: [userId], references: [id])
  imagePath  String
  disease    String
  confidence Float
  createdAt  DateTime @default(now())
}
```

5.1 Performance Evaluation Metrics
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
The classification performance is mathematically evaluated using Precision, Recall, and F1-Score:
- **Precision**: Measures the ratio of true positive predictions to the total predicted positives:
  $$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$
- **Recall**: Measures the ratio of true positive predictions to the total actual positives:
  $$\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$$
- **F1-Score**: Evaluates the harmonic mean of precision and recall:
  $$\text{F1-Score} = 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$$

The final evaluation on the combined validation set yielded a weighted average Precision, Recall, and F1-Score of 0.99. This demonstrates that the custom model maintains a strong balance between precision and recall across all 38 classes.

[Font: Times New Roman, 14pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
5.2 Explainable AI (XAI) via Grad-CAM Visualizations
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]
To make the model's predictions transparent, Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) is integrated into the classification pipeline. Grad-CAM computes the gradients of the score for a target class with respect to the feature map activations of the final convolutional layer in the last AgriBlock. These gradients are globally pooled to weigh the importance of each feature map. A ReLU activation is then applied to isolate the features that positively contribute to the target class:
$$L^c_{\text{Grad-CAM}} = \text{ReLU}\left(\sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k\right)$$

Where $A^k$ represents the $k$-th feature map activation of the last convolutional layer, and $\alpha_k^c$ represents the weight capturing the importance of feature map $k$ for class $c$. The resulting heatmap is overlaid onto the original input image. Visual inspection of these heatmaps confirms that the model focuses on the actual lesion spots on the leaf rather than background elements like soil or shadow.

5.3 Conclusion and Future Work
[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 Spacing, Justified]

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
5.3.1 Summary of Findings
The AgriVision 2.0 system successfully demonstrates that a lightweight, custom CNN architecture can achieve high classification accuracy while remaining computationally efficient. By leveraging mixed-domain training, the model's sensitivity to real-world background noise was reduced, achieving a validation accuracy of 96.14% on complex field images. The integration of a local RAG engine ensures that users receive verified, non-hallucinated treatment recommendations sourced directly from crop manuals.

[Font: Times New Roman, 12pt, Bold, Left Aligned]
5.3.2 Future Scope
Several avenues for future improvement remain:
1. **Edge Deployment**: Converting the AgriNet model to ONNX or TensorFlow Lite formats to allow offline inference directly on mobile devices.
2. **Severity Estimation**: Modifying the architecture to support dual-task learning by adding a regression head to estimate the percentage of diseased leaf area.
3. **Drone Integration**: Interfacing the system with drone-captured aerial imagery to monitor large-scale crop health across entire farms.
4. **Multilingual Interface**: Translating the diagnostic outputs and RAG recommendations into local regional languages (e.g., Hindi, Marathi, Telugu) to make the system accessible to rural farmers.
